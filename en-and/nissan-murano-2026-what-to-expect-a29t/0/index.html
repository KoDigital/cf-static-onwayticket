Nissan Murano 2026: What to Expect

Nissan’s Murano has long been the brand’s comfort-first, style-forward midsize SUV—a choice for drivers who care as much about quiet cruising as they do cargo space. With a new model on the horizon, shoppers are watching closely for meaningful updates: sharper design, smarter tech, and a powertrain that fits today’s market. Here’s a clear guide for U.S. buyers.

Redesign outlook and timing

The Murano has aged gracefully, but the segment has moved fast—especially on interior tech and efficiency. Nissan has hinted at a broader design refresh across its lineup, and the Murano feels overdue for a rethink that aligns with newer styling cues.

In real life, that likely means a leaner, more modern shape with lighting that looks more technical than decorative. The bigger question is packaging. The Murano traditionally splits the difference between two-row crossovers and three-row family haulers, and a successful redesign would double down on what it does best: comfortable five-seat travel, with smarter day-to-day usability (storage, cupholders, and phone placement).

If you like the Murano’s premise but want the latest cabin and driver-assist hardware, waiting could pay off—just stay flexible on exact timing and final U.S. specs until Nissan goes official.

Powertrain questions: gas, hybrid, or more?

The big question—will the 2026 Murano offer a hybrid—matters because midsize SUV buyers now expect efficiency options without stepping up to a premium badge. Nissan has been expanding electrification elsewhere, but what shows up in the U.S. is never guaranteed.

For now, treat hybrid talk as “possible, not promised.” Nissan could stick with a traditional gas setup to keep pricing competitive, or it could add a hybrid variant to meet market pressure from rivals. If a hybrid arrives, the goal probably won’t be sports-car quickness; it’ll be smoother low-speed response, better city efficiency, and a calmer feel in traffic—right in line with the Murano’s identity.

The practical move: decide whether hybrid is a must-have or a nice-to-have before you shop, so you’re not forcing a compromise later.

Interior upgrades and technology

The Murano has always been a “nice place to be,” and interior updates may be the most important battleground. Expect Nissan to focus on cleaner screen integration, faster software, better phone mirroring, and nicer materials where you actually touch—armrests, door uppers, and seat trim.

Most shoppers care about three things here: infotainment speed, driver assistance, and everyday convenience. Look for more USB-C ports, improved wireless charging placement, and a simpler layout that still keeps physical controls where they matter (volume and climate).

Driver-assistance upgrades are also likely. Nissan’s ProPILOT suite has evolved over time, and a refreshed Murano would ideally feel more natural on highway drives—steady lane-centering, smoother speed changes, and less “busy” correction.

Engine performance and the way it drives

For many buyers, performance isn’t about numbers; it’s about how the SUV behaves in real U.S. driving—highway merges, two-lane passing, and long-distance comfort. The Murano’s reputation has leaned toward a relaxed, quiet ride, prioritizing smoothness over aggressive tuning.

If Nissan keeps a conventional powertrain, watch for improvements in responsiveness and refinement: how quickly it answers the throttle, how it sounds when pushed, and whether the transmission behavior feels natural. A redesign could also bring chassis tweaks for more consistent steering and better composure over broken pavement, without sacrificing the Murano’s comfort-first mission.

All-wheel drive availability will still matter for snow states and wet-weather regions, even if you don’t “need” AWD every day.

Pricing, trims, and the reliability outlook

Yes, it’s worth tracking price and trims early, because value is where the Murano can either regain momentum or get squeezed. A redesign usually nudges prices upward, and Nissan will have to justify any increase with clearly better tech, cabin quality, and standard safety equipment.

Trim strategy matters. The sweet spot for many households is a mid-grade model that bundles comfort features—heated seats, upgraded audio, better driver assistance—without tipping into luxury pricing. Pay close attention to how options are packaged, since confusing bundles can push buyers into higher trims than planned.

As for reliability, the only responsible view this early is cautious. Major updates can bring new hardware and software, and early build years sometimes see more service bulletins as details get sorted. If you buy early, prioritize warranty coverage and stay on top of maintenance; if you keep cars for a decade, waiting for a year or two of owner data may be the calmer bet.

How it stacks up against rivals

The Murano doesn’t compete in a vacuum. Cross-shops like the Hyundai Santa Fe come up because both target mainstream families who want comfort, safety tech, and a modern interior without paying luxury-brand prices. The Santa Fe has leaned into bold styling and feature content; the Murano’s calling card has been a quieter, more upscale ride feel for the money.

So, is it worth waiting? If you’re drawn to a calm cabin, supportive seats, and a design that feels more premium than utilitarian, waiting makes sense—especially if Nissan delivers a meaningful tech leap and a more efficient powertrain option. If you need a vehicle immediately, today’s competitors are strong, and driving them back-to-back is still the best reality check.
